Journal Entry: Experiment 001

Project: Symbiosis of Human and Plant DNA

Objective: To integrate plant DNA into human biology, allowing photosynthesis as an alternative source of sustenance and energy, thereby eliminating the need for traditional food consumption.

On January 15, 2000, I embarked on what was supposed to be the dawn of a new era for humanity—Experiment 001. The goal was clear: to combine the remarkable photosynthetic abilities of plants with the intellectual superiority of humans. If successful, this would be the end of hunger, the birth of a self-sustaining species. But I soon discovered that merging two vastly different life forms was a task fraught with dangers I had not anticipated.

The early stages were promising. The plant cells integrated into the human genome faster than expected, responding well to stimuli. The first subject exhibited remarkable resilience, the skin turning a soft green as chlorophyll began to form. By the third week, the subject could produce energy from sunlight alone. It seemed like a breakthrough.

However, my elation was short-lived. Around week four, the symbiotic relationship started to unravel. The human immune system, once silent, began rejecting the foreign plant DNA with increasing aggression. The skin, once vibrant, thickened into a bark-like texture, covering the body in a grotesque, stiff shell. The photosynthesis, once promising, now consumed the body’s nutrients faster than it could produce them. What was meant to be a life-giving process turned parasitic.

The subject's body began to swell uncontrollably as plant tissues overgrew, overriding human functions. I watched in horror as my creation, once the epitome of scientific progress, became a prisoner inside a shell of its own making—a creature no longer human, nor plant, but something in between. Movement became impossible, respiration halted, and the once-promising symbiosis became an endless loop of growth and decay.

Despite every effort to stabilize the subject, nothing worked. The photosynthetic process was too voracious. By the end of the sixth week, the subject was little more than an overgrown statue, unable to move or communicate—trapped in its own body, still alive but suffering.

I was forced to terminate the experiment. The cost of failure was too great. This was no longer an experiment in human advancement but a descent into madness. Symbiosis, it seems, comes at a far greater price than I could have imagined.

Lessons learned: Merging human and plant DNA is possible, but the balancing act between two forms of life requires far more precision than anticipated. I will not be deterred. The future will require further experimentation, but I have glimpsed the potential. Next time, the balance will not fail.
