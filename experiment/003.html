Journal Entry: Experiment 003

Project: Avian Regeneration Trial

Objective: To integrate avian regenerative capabilities into human physiology, aiming to endow humans with the ability to regenerate lost or damaged organs and tissues, inspired by the remarkable healing processes of birds.

On March 10, 2002, I initiated Experiment 003 with high hopes of bridging the gap between avian and human regenerative capacities. The plan was to implant avian regenerative genes into human subjects, hoping to mirror the ability of birds to regrow feathers, repair injuries, and recover from substantial damage. The initial results seemed promising, as the gene therapy appeared to take hold and integrate seamlessly with the human genome.

The early stages of the experiment showed some success. The subjects exhibited increased cellular regeneration, with minor wounds healing at an accelerated rate. This progress was encouraging, suggesting that the avian genes were effectively enhancing the human body's natural repair mechanisms. However, as the experiment progressed, unforeseen complications began to emerge.

By the fourth week, the subjects began experiencing severe side effects. While the regeneration process was indeed accelerated, it was not selective. The subjects' bodies started growing additional, unnecessary tissue in random areas—flesh and skin bulges forming where they were not needed, resembling bizarre growths akin to malformed feathers. These growths, while regenerative in nature, caused immense physical and psychological discomfort.

As the weeks went on, the situation deteriorated further. The regenerative abilities began to spiral out of control, with subjects developing new organs and appendages in unpredictable locations. Some grew rudimentary wings on their backs, while others experienced uncontrolled tissue expansion around their limbs. The constant and erratic growth led to severe pain and disfigurement, pushing the subjects to the brink of despair.

The final blow came when a subject’s newly formed regenerative tissue began to turn against them, becoming hostile and aggressive. The uncontrolled growth led to internal bleeding and severe infections. Despite my attempts to stabilize the condition with additional treatments and medications, the subjects' bodies could no longer handle the invasive tissue proliferation. They deteriorated rapidly, with severe physiological and psychological breakdowns.

By the end of the experiment, I was left with the bitter realization that the avian regenerative abilities could not be harmonized with human biology without catastrophic results. The subjects' bodies, once the epitome of potential, had become grotesque mockeries of their former selves. They were neither fully human nor truly avian, but something horrific and unmanageable.

This experiment has taught me the perilous nature of attempting to integrate complex biological systems. Regenerative abilities, while powerful, must be approached with extreme caution. The blending of avian and human physiology has proven to be more destructive than beneficial. Moving forward, I must reassess the feasibility of such endeavors and consider the ethical implications of my research.
